HISTORY

The Sierra Madre in the Philippines is intertwined with the geological and cultural development of the region. Formed through tectonic activities millions of years ago, the mountain range has played a crucial role in shaping the landscape and ecosystems of northeastern Luzon. Indigenous communities have inhabited the Sierra Madre for centuries, relying on its rich natural resources for their livelihoods and maintaining a deep connection to the land. During the Spanish colonial period, the mountains served as both a refuge and a barrier, with many groups using its dense forests for protection against colonizers. In the 20th century, the Sierra Madre became a focal point for logging and agricultural expansion, leading to significant deforestation and environmental degradation. The post-war period saw increased infrastructure development, further impacting the region's ecology. Today, the Sierra Madre is recognized not only for its ecological significance but also for its cultural heritage, as efforts to conserve its unique biodiversity and protect the rights of indigenous peoples continue to evolve amid ongoing environmental challenges.

The Sierra Madre Mountain range, dubbed as the Backbone of Luzon, is also the last home to many of Philippines’ indigenous groups.
In total, over 15 million indigenous people live in the mountain range. Some of the tribes, including the Kankanaey, the Agta, the Dumagat, the Ifugao, the Kalanguya, the Isnai, and the Bugkalot people.

The Sierra Madre Oriental's rivers, including the Moctezuma and Pánuco, have captured much of the central plateau, aided by a drainage canal opened in 1607 and expanded in 1900.
These rivers offer potential for hydroelectric development, and agriculture is dense in Veracruz.
In contrast, the Sierra Madre Occidental's rugged terrain has hindered east-west communication, with Spanish exploration, commercial development, and transportation evolving separately in different regions.
The area remains sparsely populated, with settlement focused on mining and small farming communities.
The Sierra Madre Occidental provides vital water resources for both plateau oases and coastal farming regions.
