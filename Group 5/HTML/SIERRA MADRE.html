One of the Philippines’ Last Great Forests

HOME

Discover the wonders of the Sierra Madre in the Philippines, a breathtaking mountain range filled with natural beauty and rich history! Known as the "backbone of Luzon," this lush, verdant stretch is home to scenic landscapes, waterfalls, and vibrant biodiversity that thrill adventurers and nature lovers alike. Visit the famed Daranak Falls, the enchanting Masungi Georeserve, or embark on a trek to explore the stunning rainforests. The Sierra Madre also plays a vital role in Philippine history and ecology, as it stands as a crucial natural barrier against typhoons, protecting communities and preserving unique species. Come, immerse yourself in the Sierra Madre’s charm, and experience its serene beauty and ecological significance firsthand!

The Sierra Madre mountains captivate with their raw, untamed beauty, stretching along Luzon’s eastern coast like a green, mystical spine. Rising high above the horizon, its peaks are often shrouded in delicate mists that dance around the ancient trees, giving the entire landscape a dreamy, ethereal quality. The dense, emerald-colored forests teem with life, where towering trees intertwine to form a lush canopy, while beams of sunlight pierce through to highlight vibrant ferns and colorful orchids below. Hidden waterfalls cascade down rocky cliffs, their waters sparkling in the sunlight, creating natural pools that look like jewels embedded in the mountainside.

The mountains' slopes reveal sweeping views of valleys below, with rivers weaving their way through the terrain, bringing a peaceful sound of flowing water that adds to the serene ambiance. In the early morning, the Sierra Madre takes on a soft, pastel glow as the sun rises, casting shades of pink and gold across the forested hillsides. The variety of plants and flowers paints a vibrant palette that shifts with each season, reflecting the Sierra Madre's dynamic and ever-changing beauty. Wild birds and butterflies glide gracefully through the air, adding a lively yet calming presence that makes the mountains feel alive. Simply put, the Sierra Madre is not just a mountain range; it’s a natural masterpiece, an enchanting world where every peak, valley, and stream tells a story of timeless beauty and tranquility.


About Sierra Madre

Stretching from Cagayan to Quezon, Sierra Madre Mountain Range is truly the longest mountain range in the Philippines. It covers 10 provinces and stands proudly at 6,069 feet. It contains the largest remaining tract of old-growth tropical rainforest in the Philippines which is about 1.4 million hectares of forest, representing 40% of the country’s forest cover.
The Sierra Madre forests are home to hundreds of wildlife species, many of which are unique to the Philippines, including the Philippine eagle and golden crowned flying fox. It is not only rich in species diversity and endemism, but also home, pharmacy and more importantly a sacred ground and cathedral to many indigenous people.
It is also one of the major drivers of the Philippine economy, as its watersheds provide services that support major infrastructure, including dams that irrigate thousands of hectares of farm lands in Central Luzon and the Cagayan Valley region, as well as water utilities and power plants that supply Manila, the capital and industrial center of the Philippines. It serves as a typhoon barrier, attenuating incoming typhoons from the Pacific Ocean before reaching the central mainland.
No wilderness, in the Philippines, equals the Sierra Madre, with its vast and rare swath that stretches from the mountains to the ocean. But the ecological interdependence of its terrestrial, coastal, and marine ecosystems hangs in a fragile balance and faces the risk of extinction.
The Sierra Madre mountain range forest habitat is threatened by human activities. Human activities like illegal logging, mining, conversion of forest for agriculture, and migration threatened the Sierra Madre mountain range.
It continues to experience forest degradation despite the national and international campaign to preserve the environment. Logging remains the primary reason for this sad state of Sierra Madre. In addition, human encroachment and associated timber poaching, mining, conversion of forests for agriculture, and migration as well as development, particularly, road construction that will improve access and subsequent potential for exploitation of the area and proposed construction of more mega-dams also threaten its entire ecosystem and the lives of its stakeholders. By and large, corruption, poor implementation of environmental and forest protection laws, and lack of alternative livelihood for many poor people contribute to its continued degradation.

HISTORY

The Sierra Madre in the Philippines is intertwined with the geological and cultural development of the region. Formed through tectonic activities millions of years ago, the mountain range has played a crucial role in shaping the landscape and ecosystems of northeastern Luzon. Indigenous communities have inhabited the Sierra Madre for centuries, relying on its rich natural resources for their livelihoods and maintaining a deep connection to the land. During the Spanish colonial period, the mountains served as both a refuge and a barrier, with many groups using its dense forests for protection against colonizers. In the 20th century, the Sierra Madre became a focal point for logging and agricultural expansion, leading to significant deforestation and environmental degradation. The post-war period saw increased infrastructure development, further impacting the region's ecology. Today, the Sierra Madre is recognized not only for its ecological significance but also for its cultural heritage, as efforts to conserve its unique biodiversity and protect the rights of indigenous peoples continue to evolve amid ongoing environmental challenges.

The Sierra Madre Mountain range, dubbed as the Backbone of Luzon, is also the last home to many of Philippines’ indigenous groups.
In total, over 15 million indigenous people live in the mountain range. Some of the tribes, including the Kankanaey, the Agta, the Dumagat, the Ifugao, the Kalanguya, the Isnai, and the Bugkalot people.

The Sierra Madre Oriental's rivers, including the Moctezuma and Pánuco, have captured much of the central plateau, aided by a drainage canal opened in 1607 and expanded in 1900.
These rivers offer potential for hydroelectric development, and agriculture is dense in Veracruz.
In contrast, the Sierra Madre Occidental's rugged terrain has hindered east-west communication, with Spanish exploration, commercial development, and transportation evolving separately in different regions.
The area remains sparsely populated, with settlement focused on mining and small farming communities.
The Sierra Madre Occidental provides vital water resources for both plateau oases and coastal farming regions.


TOP ATTRACTION

1. Ditumabo Falls (Aurora)

Also known as the "Mother Falls," Ditumabo Falls is a majestic waterfall located in San Luis, Aurora, near Baler. It features a powerful cascade of cool, clear water surrounded by lush greenery and towering rock formations. The falls are reached after a scenic 45-minute trek through forests, small streams, and boulders.

How to go to this tourist spot?

From Manila: Take a bus bound for Baler (Genesis or JoyBus) at Cubao or Pasay terminals (6-7 hours). From Baler town proper, hire a tricycle to take you to the jump-off point of Ditumabo Falls in Barangay Ditumabo, San Luis (about 30 minutes). You’ll need to trek for around 45 minutes to reach the falls. Its natural beauty and less-crowded atmosphere make it a hidden gem in the region.

2. Angat Watershed (Bulacan)

The Angat Watershed is a protected area that supplies water to Metro Manila and irrigates farmlands in Bulacan and Pampanga. It is also the site of Angat Dam, a major water reservoir. The watershed is home to diverse wildlife and dense forests, making it an important ecological zone. Its natural beauty and less-crowded atmosphere make it a hidden gem in the region.

How to go to this tourist spot?

From Manila: Take a bus bound for Norzagaray, Bulacan from Cubao or Monumento (2 hours). Upon reaching Norzagaray, hire a tricycle or a jeepney to take you to Angat Dam and the watershed area, which is within the Ipo Watershed Reserve. Be sure to arrange permits and guides beforehand. Its natural beauty and less-crowded atmosphere make it a hidden gem in the region.

3. Dingalan Bay (Aurora)

Dingalan Bay is located on the eastern coast of Luzon, in Aurora Province. Known for its rugged cliffs, scenic beaches, and breathtaking views of the Pacific Ocean, Dingalan is often referred to as the "Batanes of the East." Popular activities include hiking to the lighthouse, exploring white-sand beaches, and boat rides around the bay.

How to go to this tourist spot?

From Manila: Take a bus bound for Cabanatuan (3-4 hours). From Cabanatuan City terminal, transfer to a van or bus going to Dingalan (2-3 hours). Once in Dingalan town proper, you can take a tricycle or a boat to various scenic spots around Dingalan Bay exploring white-sand beaches, and boat rides around the bay.

4. Mount Daraitan (Rizal)

Mount Daraitan is a popular hiking destination in Tanay, Rizal, Sierra Madre mountain range and the Tinipak River. The mountain is known for its limestone formations, caves, and clear river pools. The climb is moderately challenging, attracting nature lovers and adventurers looking for a quick escape from the city.

How to go to this tourist spot?

From Manila: Ride a jeepney or UV Express from Cubao or Shaw Boulevard to Tanay Public Market (1-2 hours). From there, hire a tricycle to the Barangay Daraitan jump-off point (about 1 hour). 6. Daranak Falls (Rizal)

Daranak Falls is a beautiful waterfall located in Tanay, Rizal. It cascades into a large basin surrounded by lush greenery, making it a great spot for swimming and picnicking. Easily accessible from the road, it is one of the most popular nature spots in the region, perfect for family outings and relaxation dont forget to bring your frind.

7. Masungi Georeserve (Rizal)

Masungi Georeserve is a conservation area located in Baras, Rizal, dedicated to preserving limestone formations and rich biodiversity. Visitors can experience its eco-trail featuring hanging bridges, rope courses, and scenic views of the Sierra Madre forest. It’s known for its commitment to environmental. Its natural beauty and less-crowded atmosphere make it a hidden gem in the region.

How to go to this tourist spot?

From Manila: Take a private vehicle or book a van heading toward Tanay via Marcos Highway (1-2 hours). The Masungi Georeserve entrance is along Kilometer 47 of Marcos Highway, accessible by car or hired transport. Pre-arranged tours are required. Its natural beauty and less-crowded atmosphere make it a hidden gem in the region.

8. Casapsapan Beach (Aurora)

Casapsapan Beach is a remote, pristine white-sand beach located in Casiguran, Aurora. Known for its crystal-clear waters and peaceful environment, the beach is ideal for those seeking solitude and relaxation away from the usual tourist spots. Its natural beauty and less-crowded atmosphere make it a hidden gem in the region.

How to go to this tourist spot?

From Manila: Take a bus bound for Baler (Genesis or JoyBus, 6-7 hours). From Baler, take another bus or van going to Casiguran (3-4 hours). From Casiguran town proper, hire a tricycle or boat to reach Casapsapan Beach (30-45 minutes). This area is more remote, so plan accordingly. Its natural beauty and less-crowded atmosphere make it a hidden gem in the region.
